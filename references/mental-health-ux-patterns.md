# Mental Health UX Patterns

Reference patterns for apps serving vulnerable users or touching mental health topics.
Source: [designpatternsformentalhealth.org](https://designpatternsformentalhealth.org/) — a collaborative resource by Snook, PublicPolicy Lab, and Barnardo's.

## When to apply these

Use this reference when building apps that:
- Support users experiencing mental health challenges
- Serve vulnerable populations (young people, people in crisis, caregivers)
- Handle sensitive personal data or disclosures
- Offer any kind of peer support, self-help, or wellbeing tracking

---

## Principles

| Principle | Intent |
|-----------|--------|
| **Listen and Respond** | Engage with user feedback and act on what is heard |
| **Make it Human** | Design with empathy and authentic human connection |
| **Give Control** | Empower users to direct their own experience |
| **Be Clear** | Communicate in plain, understandable language |
| **Adapt to Changing Needs** | Build flexible solutions that evolve with user circumstances |
| **Create a Safe Space** | Foster environments where users feel secure and protected |
| **Be Reliable and Consistent** | Maintain dependable, predictable interactions |

---

## Patterns

### Listen and Respond

- **Tell the story once** — Only ask users to share their story once; refer back to it rather than repeating the ask.
- **Provide a space for peer support** — Enable users to interact with one another, build solidarity and trust.
- **Provide a range of ways to express yourself** — Offer multiple input modes (text, voice, emoji, images) since users communicate differently.

### Make it Human

- **Signpost to local services** — Direct users to community resources when your service can't meet a need.
- **Manage expectations** — State clearly what the service can and cannot offer; disclose whether it is monitored.
- **Display hours of service** — Be explicit about when you can respond to users directly.
- **Clearly show how to get in touch** — Make contact routes visible so users can reach support when they need it.
- **Build positive self-image** — Use positive, inclusive imagery; avoid stereotypes of poor mental health.

### Give Control

- **Provide a visible record of progress** — Visible progress tracking builds consistency and familiarity.
- **Encourage self-care** — Include general wellbeing information alongside support content.
- **Define personal milestones** — Let users set their own goals rather than imposing fixed outcomes.

### Be Clear

- **Lead with what you offer** — State your support offering prominently so users in distress immediately understand what help is available.

### Adapt to Changing Needs

- **Ease of access** — Reduce friction at every access point so support is actually used.
- **Design for poor internet connection** — Account for limited connectivity; cache critical content offline where possible.

### Create a Safe Space

- **Tell people how you will use their information** — Proactively explain data use; don't bury it in a privacy policy.
- **Provide routes to anonymity** — Be clear what confidentiality and anonymity you can and cannot guarantee.
- **Provide an escape** — Give users a way to quickly exit or hide visible content (e.g., a panic button that navigates away).

## Checklist for mental health–sensitive features

- [ ] Crisis signposting visible without searching (helpline numbers, emergency contacts)
- [ ] No dark patterns that increase engagement at the cost of wellbeing (streaks, guilt loops)
- [ ] Consent language is plain English, not legalese
- [ ] Data retention and sharing explained in plain language, not buried
- [ ] Anonymity options surfaced — not just available if the user finds them
- [ ] Content warnings before potentially distressing material
- [ ] Quick-exit affordance on any screen showing sensitive content
- [ ] Offline / low-bandwidth fallback for critical support pathways
- [ ] Peer support spaces moderated with clear community guidelines visible
- [ ] Progress metrics are user-defined, not app-imposed
